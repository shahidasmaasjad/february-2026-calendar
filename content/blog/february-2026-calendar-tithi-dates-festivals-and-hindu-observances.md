A tithi is a lunar day, representing the time it takes for the Moon to increase its longitudinal angle by 12 degrees from the Sun. There are 30 tithis in a lunar month, divided into two phases: Shukla Paksha (the waxing phase) and Krishna Paksha (the waning phase). Each tithi has its own significance and is associated with specific activities and observances.

The Hindu calendar, also known as the Panchang, is a lunisolar calendar that incorporates both lunar and solar cycles. It is widely used in India and Nepal to determine festival dates, auspicious timings for ceremonies, and other religious observances. The Panchang includes details such as tithi, nakshatra (lunar mansion), yoga, karana, and vara (weekday).

The importance of understanding the tithi lies in its influence on daily life, particularly for those who adhere to Hindu traditions. Auspicious activities like weddings, housewarming ceremonies, and starting new ventures are often planned based on favorable tithis. Additionally, many religious fasts and festivals are observed on specific tithis.

Tithis are integral to various aspects of Hindu life, influencing the timing of religious ceremonies, festivals, and daily rituals. Different tithis are considered auspicious for different activities. For instance, some tithis are ideal for starting new projects, while others are more suitable for charitable activities or honoring ancestors.

**Shukla Paksha Tithis:** These are the tithis during the waxing phase of the Moon, from Pratipada (first day) to Purnima (full moon). They are generally considered auspicious for starting new ventures, performing religious ceremonies, and celebrating festivals.

**Krishna Paksha Tithis:** These are the tithis during the waning phase of the Moon, from Pratipada (first day after the full moon) to Amavasya (new moon). While not as universally auspicious as Shukla Paksha tithis, certain Krishna Paksha tithis are significant for specific observances, such as honoring ancestors.

**Specific Tithis:** Each tithi, such as Ekadashi, Chaturthi, and Ashtami, has its own unique significance and associated observances. For example, Ekadashi is considered highly auspicious for fasting and worshipping Lord Vishnu.

The **February 2026 calendar tithi** dates will vary based on the specific location and the Panchang being followed. Regional variations in calendars and observances are common in India. It’s important to consult a local Panchang or religious authority for the most accurate dates and timings.

Different regions and communities may observe festivals and fasts on slightly different dates due to variations in the Panchang. For instance, some communities may follow a lunar calendar that starts with the new moon (Amanta), while others follow a calendar that starts with the full moon (Purnimanta).

The Gregorian calendar dates corresponding to the tithis in the Hindu calendar will shift slightly each year due to the differences between the lunar and solar cycles. Therefore, it’s essential to refer to a specific calendar for February 2026 to get the exact dates.
