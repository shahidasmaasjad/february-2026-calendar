The Gujarati calendar, also known as the Vikram Samvat calendar in Gujarat, is a lunisolar calendar that plays a central role in the lives of Gujarati people. It meticulously tracks time based on both lunar months and solar years, making it distinct from purely lunar or purely solar calendars. Its significance extends beyond mere date-keeping, influencing religious festivals, agricultural cycles, and personal life events.

Rooted deeply in ancient Indian astronomical calculations and spiritual traditions, the Gujarati calendar begins its new year on the day after Diwali, typically in October or November. This unique starting point sets it apart from other Vikram Samvat calendars observed in different regions of India, which often begin in Chaitra (March/April).

Understanding the Gujarati calendar is vital for anyone wishing to engage deeply with Gujarati culture. It’s not just a system of dates; it’s a living tradition that guides daily life, from auspicious beginnings of new ventures to the timing of weddings and the remembrance of ancestors. The calendar is intrinsically linked to astrology, dictating propitious moments (muhurats) for various undertakings.

The core components of the Gujarati calendar include the year (Samvat), months (Maas), and various daily elements like Tithi, Paksha, Nakshatra, Yoga, and Karan. These elements are not static but shift daily, reflecting the intricate movements of celestial bodies. For 4 February 2026, each of these elements will hold specific values that inform its overall character.

While the primary Gujarati calendar follows the Vikram Samvat, it’s essential to note subtle variations. The most significant distinction within Indian lunisolar calendars is between the *Amavasyant* (ending with a new moon) and *Purnimant* (ending with a full moon) systems for defining months. Gujarat predominantly follows the Amavasyant system, where a month concludes on Amavasya (new moon day).

Regional differences within Gujarat itself, though minor, can sometimes influence the exact timing of certain festivals or local observances. These variations are usually due to local traditions or specific interpretations of astrological data, making it important to consult a reliable local Panchang if precise regional details are required.

Another notable variation is the concept of *Adhik Maas*, or an intercalary month. To reconcile the lunar year with the solar year, an extra month is added approximately every three years. This addition ensures that festivals and seasons remain aligned, a critical feature for an agricultural society. While not directly relevant to a single specific date like 4 February 2026, it’s a fundamental aspect of the calendar’s structure.

The context of time is paramount when interpreting calendar details. For 4 February 2026, we are looking at a specific point in the Gregorian calendar which translates to a particular date within the Vikram Samvat year. This year will be **Vikram Samvat 2082**, as the new year (VS 2083) would typically begin after Diwali in late 2026.

Furthermore, the precise calculations for Tithi, Nakshatra, and other elements are location-dependent. Sunrise and sunset times vary significantly across different longitudes and latitudes. Therefore, a Panchang calculated for Ahmedabad will have slightly different timings for certain astrological events compared to one calculated for, say, London or New York, even on the same Gregorian date.

Users abroad seeking Gujarati calendar details must consider their local time zone and geographical coordinates. Modern online Panchangs often allow users to specify their location, which is crucial for accurate Muhurat calculations and observance of fasts or festivals that depend on local sunrise and sunset.

* **Tithi (Lunar Day):** There are 15 Tithis in each Paksha, making 30 in a lunar month. Each Tithi has specific characteristics and is associated with certain activities. Knowing the Tithi on 4 February 2026 is the first step to understanding the day’s energy.
* **Paksha (Fortnight):** The lunar month is divided into two Pakshas: *Shukla Paksha* (waxing moon, bright fortnight) and *Krishna Paksha* (waning moon, dark fortnight). The Paksha indicates the moon’s phase and influences the general mood of the period.
* **Nakshatra (Lunar Mansion):** The zodiac is divided into 27 Nakshatras, each spanning 13 degrees 20 minutes. Nakshatras are crucial for astrological predictions, birth charts, and determining auspicious times for specific ceremonies.
* **Yoga (Lunar Day Union):** There are 27 Yogas, formed by the combined longitudes of the Sun and Moon. Each Yoga has unique properties and can influence the outcome of actions performed during its duration.
* **Karan (Half Tithi):** A Tithi is divided into two Karans. There are 11 Karans, some of which are fixed and others movable. Karans provide further refinement to the auspiciousness of a given moment.
* **Vaar (Day of the Week):** The standard seven days of the week, each associated with a planet and having its own significance.
* **Samvat (Year):** The current Vikram Samvat year, which for 4 February 2026, will be Vikram Samvat 2082.

These details empower individuals to align their actions with favorable cosmic energies, according to traditional beliefs.

When seeking information for 4 February 2026 in the Gujarati calendar, it’s important to choose reliable sources to ensure accuracy. The proliferation of online resources means that not all information is equally trustworthy.

Consider consulting reputable online Panchang websites that are known for their astrological accuracy. Look for sites that allow you to input your specific location (city, state, country) to get personalized timings for sunrise, sunset, and the exact start/end of Tithis and Nakshatras.

Traditional printed Gujarati calendars and almanacs (Panchangs) published by well-known religious institutions or astrologers are also highly reliable. These often include extensive details, including festival lists, auspicious timings, and planetary positions.

A common mistake to avoid is relying on generic calendar apps or websites that do not specify their calculation methodology or source. Always cross-reference information from at least two credible sources, especially for critical events like weddings or religious ceremonies.

**Are there any major festivals on 4 February 2026?** While 4 February is generally in the middle of the Gregorian year, major Gujarati festivals typically fall in different months. It is less likely to be a major festival day, but a detailed calendar would confirm any minor observances or fasts.

**What is the significance of the Gujarati calendar?** The Gujarati calendar is significant because it guides religious practices, cultural celebrations, and personal auspicious timings for millions of Gujarati people worldwide. It connects them to their heritage and spiritual traditions.

**Is the Gujarati calendar the same as other Indian calendars?** While sharing the Vikram Samvat system, the Gujarati calendar primarily uses the Amavasyant system for month endings and begins its new year after Diwali, which distinguishes it from some other regional Indian calendars.
