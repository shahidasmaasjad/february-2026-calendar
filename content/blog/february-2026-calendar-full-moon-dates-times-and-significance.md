A full moon occurs when the Moon is exactly opposite the Sun in its orbit around Earth, making its entire face illuminated from our perspective. This alignment results in the Moon appearing as a perfect, luminous disc in the night sky. While it appears full for about three days, the moment of ‘fullness’ is a precise instant when 100% of its face is illuminated.

The lunar cycle, or synodic month, averages about 29.5 days. This means that full moons typically occur roughly once a month, though their exact date shifts slightly from year to year. Calendars that track these phases are invaluable for those who rely on or simply enjoy observing the Moon’s journey through the sky.

For 2026, the February full moon holds particular interest as it ushers in the late winter period in the Northern Hemisphere. Understanding its position within the broader lunar calendar helps contextualize its traditional names and associated folklore, which often reflect seasonal changes and historical observations.

While all full moons share the common characteristic of being fully illuminated, they can be categorized or observed with slightly different attributes. One common distinction is the phenomenon of a *Supermoon* or *Micromoon*. A Supermoon occurs when the full moon coincides with the Moon being at its closest point to Earth (perigee) in its orbit, making it appear larger and brighter. Conversely, a Micromoon happens at apogee, its furthest point.

Another variation relates to traditional naming conventions. Different cultures and regions have historically assigned unique names to each full moon of the year, often reflecting seasonal changes, agricultural cycles, or significant events. These names, while not astronomical classifications, add a rich layer of cultural context to lunar observation.

Occasionally, a full moon might also coincide with a lunar eclipse, where the Earth passes directly between the Sun and Moon, casting a shadow on the lunar surface. While not every full moon features an eclipse, these events are significant astronomical occurrences that draw considerable public attention and offer spectacular viewing opportunities.

The timing of the full moon can vary significantly depending on your geographical location and time zone. The astronomical moment of full illumination is a universal instant, but its local date and clock time will differ across the globe. For instance, a full moon occurring at midnight UTC might fall on the previous day in some Western time zones and the next day in Eastern ones.

Historically, the interpretation and use of full moon dates have also evolved. Ancient civilizations relied heavily on lunar cycles for agriculture, navigation, and religious festivals. Today, while scientific precision has replaced some of these practical dependencies, the cultural and spiritual significance of the full moon persists, influencing modern traditions and personal practices.

Furthermore, different calendars and astronomical models may present slightly varied times for the full moon, though these differences are usually minor and related to precision in calculation. It is always wise to consult reliable astronomical sources to ensure the most accurate data for your specific location.

When is the **february 2026 calendar full moon**?

The full moon in February 2026 will occur on *Wednesday, February 11, 2026, at 1:32 PM UTC* (Coordinated Universal Time). You will need to convert this time to your local time zone to determine the exact moment it becomes full in your area.

What is the traditional name for the February full moon?

The traditional name for the February full moon in many Northern Hemisphere cultures is the *Snow Moon*. This name reflects the heavy snowfalls typically experienced during this month, making hunting difficult and often leading to scarcity.

Will the February 2026 full moon be a Supermoon?

No, the February 2026 full moon is not expected to be a Supermoon. A Supermoon occurs when the full moon coincides with the Moon’s closest approach to Earth (perigee). The February 2026 full moon will occur when the Moon is not at its perigee.

How do full moons affect tides?

Full moons, along with new moons, create stronger tidal forces known as *spring tides*. During these phases, the gravitational pulls of the Sun and Moon combine to create higher high tides and lower low tides. This is due to their aligned positions relative to Earth.

Is the full moon visible everywhere on Earth at the same time?

The astronomical moment of the full moon is universal, but its visibility and local date/time will vary. If the full moon occurs during daylight hours in your location, you won’t see it until sunset, and it may appear on a different calendar date depending on your time zone.

Why do full moon dates change each year?

Full moon dates shift annually because the lunar cycle (approximately 29.5 days) is shorter than the average calendar month. This discrepancy causes the full moon to occur a little earlier each successive calendar month, leading to a different date each year.
